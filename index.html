Sculpture

Sculpture is one of the oldest and most influential branches of fine art. It deals with the creation of three-dimensional forms using various materials such as stone, wood, bronze, metal, clay, marble, plastic, and others. Sculpture differs from painting in that it has a real, tangible volume and spatial existence — it can be viewed from different angles, walked around, and physically experienced.

Sculpture can be round sculpture (fully three-dimensional, visible from all sides) or relief sculpture, when the image partially emerges from a flat surface (for example, sculptures applied to building facades or monuments).

The history of sculpture begins in prehistoric times, when ancient humans made small stone or bone figurines as ritual or cult objects. In Ancient Egypt, Mesopotamia, Greece, and Rome, sculpture reached a high level of development, serving religious, state, and aesthetic purposes. Greek sculpture is especially renowned for its ideal proportions, its study of the beauty of the human body, and the expression of movement. From the Middle Ages onward, sculpture acquired a religious character, particularly in the decoration of cathedrals and churches. During the Renaissance, it once again became a reflection of the beauty of humanity and nature — thanks to masters like Michelangelo.

Armenian Sculpture

Armenian sculpture has a history spanning thousands of years. It began during the Urartian period (9th–6th centuries BC), when artists created bronze figurines of animals and deities, high reliefs, and architectural compositions.

In ancient Armenia, the sculptures found on temples, tombs, and fortresses often served religious purposes. They depicted gods, heroes, animals, or symbolic motifs. For instance, the sculptural columns and ceramic decorations of the Garni pagan temple testify to the high artistic taste of that era.

After the adoption of Christianity in 301 AD, Armenian sculpture gained a new spiritual content. A unique phenomenon emerged in Christian art — the khachkar (cross-stone): a carved stone monument shaped as a cross, which served as a religious, aesthetic, and national symbol. The fine engravings on khachkars — with intricate floral patterns, complex compositions, and diverse cross designs — became the most vivid expression of Armenian sculpture.

Among the famous medieval masters was Momik (14th century), who was not only a sculptor but also an architect and miniaturist. His works, such as the khachkars of Noravank Monastery, are considered masterpieces of medieval Armenian sculpture.

In modern times, Armenian sculpture continued to develop, embracing new materials, forms, and ideas. During the 20th century, renowned masters such as Ervand Kochar, Arpiar Sasouni, Getik Baghdasaryan, and Grigor Khanjyan (also known for monumental works) emerged. Ervand Kochar, in particular, became famous for his concept of “Man and the Universe” and his kinetic sculptures — a synthesis of sculpture and time.

Today, Armenian sculptors create both monumental statues dedicated to historical figures (for example, Mesrop Mashtots, Hovhannes Tumanyan, Alexander Spendiaryan, and others) and contemporary artworks exhibited in international biennales and art shows.
